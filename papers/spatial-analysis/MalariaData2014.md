# A national health facility survey of malaria infection among febrile patients in Kenya, 2014

**Authors:** Sophie Githinji, Abdisalan M. Noor, Josephine Malinga, Peter M. Macharia, Rebecca Kiptui, Ahmeddin Omar, Kiambo Njagi, Ejersa Waqo, Robert W. Snow
**Journal:** Malaria Journal | **Year:** 2016 | **DOI:** 10.1186/s12936-016-1638-2
**File:** papers/spatial-analysis/MalariaData2014.md

## Abstract

The use of malaria infection prevalence among febrile patients at health facilities has potential as a valuable epidemiological surveillance tool, but routine data are often incomplete and not all fevers are tested. This study was designed to screen all febrile patients for malaria infection across Kenya to explore the epidemiology of fever test positivity rates (TPR). A national cross-sectional survey was conducted using random spatial sampling within five malaria epidemiological zones: high lake endemic (Lake Victoria), moderate coast endemic, highland epidemic, seasonal low transmission, and low risk. From 234 public health facilities (23 hospitals, 99 health centres, 112 dispensaries), 10,557 consenting febrile out-patients without prior anti-malarial treatment were enrolled during October–November 2014 (short rainy season) and screened using CareStart HRP2 *P. falciparum* RDTs.

Overall, 1,633 (15.5%) febrile patients were RDT positive at 124 (53.0%) facilities. Infection prevalence among non-pregnant patients varied dramatically by zone: from 0.6% in the low risk zone to 41.6% in the high lake endemic zone. At 110 (47%) facilities, no RDT-positive febrile patients were found. Age patterns of infection reflected transmission intensity: in the lake endemic zone, 6% of infections were among children <1 year compared to 0% in low risk zones. Test positivity among febrile pregnant women was 31.4% in the lake endemic zone versus 0% in the low risk zone. The study demonstrates that TPR from health facility data, when collected systematically with patient age, pregnancy status, and location, can provide invaluable epidemiological data on spatial heterogeneity of malaria and serve as a cost-effective complement to expensive community-based cross-sectional surveys for planning and monitoring malaria control.

## Methods

- Stratified spatial sampling framework from 4,242 geo-coded public health facilities across five malaria epidemiological zones in Kenya
- Random spatially weighted sample proportionate to number and types of health facilities within each zone
- Target: ≥50 febrile patients per facility (234 facilities required across all zones)
- 76 senior laboratory technologists trained on survey procedures and RDT diagnosis (CareStart HRP2 *P. falciparum*)
- Patients with history of fever in last 48 hours, no prior anti-malarial treatment, informed consent
- Data on age, sex, pregnancy status, and usual residence collected
- Data entry in Microsoft Access; analysis in STATA v12

## Key Results

- 10,557 febrile patients screened at 234 public health facilities; 1,633 (15.5%) RDT positive
- 124/234 (53.0%) facilities had at least one positive case; 110 (47%) had zero positives
- TPR by zone (non-pregnant): lake endemic 41.6%, coast endemic 22.5%, highland epidemic 13.9%, seasonal low 3.4%, low risk 0.6%
- Peak TPR in children aged 5–9 years (28.6%) across all zones
- Lake endemic zone: 31.4% TPR among pregnant women (16/51)
- Low risk zone: 0% TPR in infants <1 year and pregnant women
- Notable shift of peak parasite prevalence from younger to older children, attributed to enhanced control reducing immunity acquisition
- 6% of all infections in lake endemic zone among children <1 year

## Relevance to MalariaSentinel (Centinela)

This study demonstrates the utility of health facility-based test positivity rates (TPR) as a surveillance tool for characterising spatial heterogeneity in malaria risk — a core function of the Centinela SDSS. The stratified sampling approach across epidemiological zones provides a template for the Centinela's data collection framework. The finding that routine data quality limitations (incomplete testing, aggregation losing age/location granularity) can be addressed through systematic surveys directly informs the Centinela's data quality assessment pipeline. The paper's demonstration that TPR patterns mirror community-based prevalence supports the Centinela's use of facility data for risk stratification.

Background
The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends that all persons suspected of malaria should be examined for evidence of Plasmodium infection by either microscopy or rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) before treatment is initiated [1–5]. Since the adoption of this global policy in 2010, the use of RDTs has doubled in Africa [6, 7]. Kenya adopted the policy on universal parasitological diagnosis in 2010 [8] and in 2012 embarked on a plan of rolling out RDTs [9]. The use of fever test positivity rates (TPR) has a long history as a measure of malaria risk in communities [10, 11]. The present study aimed to characterize TPR in patients with reported fever presenting to clinics across Kenya to define patterns of febrile infection rates nationwide as a potential epidemiological surveillance tool.
Methods
A national cross-sectional survey was undertaken at public health facilities sampled according to malaria endemicity zones: lake endemic (western Kenya around Lake Victoria), coast endemic (Kenyan coast), highland epidemic (highlands west of Rift Valley), seasonal low transmission (northern, eastern and southeastern Kenya), and low risk (central highlands including Nairobi). A spatial sampling design tool was used to draw a random spatially weighted sample proportionate to number and types of health facilities within each zone. With a target of 50 febrile patients per facility, 234 public health facilities were required across all zones. The survey was conducted in October and November 2014, coinciding with the short rainy season.
Results
The survey was conducted at all 234 sampled health facilities. A total of 58,080 out-patients presented during the survey days; 12,913 (22.2%) reported a history of fever within the last 48 hours; 10,557 were enrolled. Overall, 1,633 (15.5%) febrile patients were RDT positive at 124 (53.0%) facilities. Among non-pregnant patients, infection prevalence ranged from 0.6% in the low risk zone to 41.6% in the high lake endemic zone. The age pattern of infection across all zones showed low rates in infants rising to peak in children aged 5–9 and/or 10–14 years, then declining through young adulthood. In the lake endemic zone, 31.4% of febrile pregnant women were RDT positive.
Discussion and Conclusions
The study demonstrates that TPR recorded at public health facilities varied between malaria endemic zones in ways that could operationally define malaria risk. Traditional high transmission areas around Lake Victoria showed the highest TPR, highest rates in infants, and highest rates among pregnant women. The default for classifying malaria risk zones in Africa continues to be community-based cross-sectional surveys [29], which are expensive. Conversely, testing of all fevers at health facilities should be routine and available at no additional cost. Routine continuous data from facilities have the advantage of covering every month of every year. However, the detail, completeness and coverage of routine data necessary for reliable epidemiological analysis remains poor [37]. Malaria infection rates among febrile patients could provide invaluable epidemiological data on seasonal, temporal and spatial heterogeneity of malaria and serve as replacements to more expensive community-based infection rates.
