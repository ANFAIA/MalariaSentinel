# Modelling Anopheles gambiae mosquito population dynamics in heterogeneous landscapes

## Abstract

This PhD thesis develops a generalisable mathematical framework for modelling *Anopheles gambiae* population dynamics in heterogeneous landscapes, incorporating environmental dependencies (temperature, rainfall) with mosquito life history traits. The framework is validated against adult mosquito abundance data from Burkina Faso and Nigeria, demonstrating key mechanisms driving population dynamics changes across diverse environmental conditions.

## Methods

- Generalisable mathematical framework combining differential equations with agent-based components
- Environmental dependencies: temperature-dependent development rates, rainfall-driven habitat availability
- Mosquito life history traits: fecundity, mortality, gonotrophic周期 duration
- Validation against entomological survey data from Burkina Faso (Dielmo/Ndiop) and Nigeria
- Sensitivity analysis of environmental and biological parameters
- Landscape heterogeneity modelling via spatially varying parameter fields

## Key Results

- Environmental drivers (temperature, rainfall) explain majority of temporal variance in mosquito abundance
- Rainfall has delayed effect on population dynamics through aquatic habitat filling/emptying
- Temperature creates upper and lower bounds on development rates and adult survival
- Model successfully predicts inter-annual population variability across two distinct West African sites
- Landscape heterogeneity significantly affects local population dynamics and spatial distribution

## Relevance to MalariaSentinel (Centinela)

Provides validated framework for incorporating environmental factors into Anopheles population modeling. The West African validation (Burkina Faso, Nigeria) is directly transferable to Ghana's ecological context. The environmental dependency parameterisation connects to the Centinela's geospatial data layers (CHIRPS rainfall, MODIS temperature). The landscape heterogeneity approach supports the Centinela's goal of spatially explicit transmission modelling at fine resolution.

## Limitations

- Thesis format — key contributions may need extraction for direct application
- Validation limited to two sites; broader African validation needed
- Does not capture An. funestus dynamics (focus on An. gambiae s.l. only)
- Larval habitat ecology simplified relative to field complexity

## Future Directions

- Extend to multi-species dynamics (An. gambiae + An. funestus)
- Incorporate land use/land cover change projections
- Couple with malaria transmission model for integrated prediction
- Validate against Ghana-specific entomological data
