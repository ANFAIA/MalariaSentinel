## Strengths of the Current Literature

### 1. Methodological Rigor in ABM Calibration
The best ABMs (EMOD, Angelakis SpatPOMP) are calibrated against multi-year field data with explicit uncertainty quantification. Mondal et al. (2025) achieved transferability across 8 African sites via neural network emulation — a significant advance for making ABMs operationally useful. The field has moved beyond proof-of-concept to validated, policy-relevant models.

### 2. Open-Source Reproducibility
Key tools are openly available: AnophelesModel R package, EMOD, Google Earth Engine pipelines, INLA/R-INLA for Bayesian spatial statistics. The Senegal hazard-vulnerability framework (Morlighem 2025) uses entirely open-source covariates. This lowers the barrier for MalariaSentinel's implementation.

### 3. Operational Validation
CIMS (Bioko Island) and eSPT (3-country pilot) demonstrate that digital SDSS platforms produce measurable operational improvements when deployed with local adaptation. These are not theoretical — they've been tested in real programmes.

### 4. Multi-Species Awareness
The literature increasingly recognizes that monospecific models are inadequate. Rakotoarison (2025), Henyimana (2026), and Fernandez Montoya (2021) all emphasize species-specific intervention response profiles. This aligns with MalariaSentinel's need for multi-vector parameterization.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

### 1. Geographic Bias: East/Southern Africa Dominance
The majority of validated ABMs and risk maps are from East Africa (Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda) or West Africa (Nigeria, Benin, Senegal). Ghana-specific ABM calibration is notably absent from the recent literature. MalariaSentinel will need to build Ghana-specific parameterization from scratch or adapt Nigerian/Senegalese calibrations.

**Risk**: Transferring model parameters across countries without validation could produce misleading risk maps.

**Mitigation**: Use the archetype-based approach from Nigeria (22 archetypes, EMOD) to identify which archetypes apply to Ghana's districts, then calibrate locally.

### 2. Temporal Resolution Gap
Most ABMs operate at monthly or seasonal resolution. The DLNM analysis (Okiring 2021) shows that temperature effects peak at lag 2 weeks — sub-monthly dynamics matter. Yet no ABM in the literature explicitly encodes weekly lag-response structures. MalariaSentinel's H1 hypothesis (lag-aware climate forcing) addresses this gap directly.

### 3. Human Mobility Underrepresented
Only Walker et al. (2026) and Angelakis et al. (2026) include human movement in their models. Most ABMs treat populations as static. For Ghana, where internal migration between northern savanna and southern urban areas drives importation, this is a critical gap. MalariaSentinel's H5 hypothesis (importation risk modeling) is well-motivated.

### 4. Intervention Cascade Incompleteness
While Champagne et al. (2025) quantified the full ITN effectiveness cascade, most ABMs still model interventions as simple coverage percentages. The gap between "80% ITN coverage" and actual population protection is substantial (usage × durability × exposure timing). MalariaSentinel should resist the temptation to model interventions as binary on/off.

### 5. Urban Malaria Neglect
Vanhuysse et al. (2023) is one of the few studies addressing urban malaria at fine scale. Yet urbanization across Africa is accelerating, and Ghana's cities (Accra, Kumasi) have growing malaria burdens. The literature provides limited guidance for urban ABM parameterization.

## Methodological Assessment: What Works and What Doesn't

### What Works
- **Bayesian geostatistical models** (INLA/SPDE) for risk mapping — well-validated, reproducible, handles spatial autocorrelation
- **Neural network emulation** of ABMs — makes real-time scenario evaluation feasible
- **Hazard-vulnerability decomposition** — conceptually clean, operationally actionable
- **Google Earth Engine** for environmental layer generation — scalable, reproducible, free

### What's Promising but Unvalidated
- **Joint vector-incidence modeling** (Gbaguidi 2026) — novel but single-study
- **Agricultural calendar integration** (Rakotoarison 2025) — mechanistically sound but only validated in Madagascar
- **Hydrological process modeling** for suitability (Smith 2024) — theoretically superior but not yet operationalized at district scale

### What Doesn't Work Well
- **Precipitation-only suitability models** — Smith et al. (2024) definitively showed these are inadequate
- **Monospecific vector models** in multi-species settings — systematically overestimate ITN impact
- **Static population models** — cannot capture importation dynamics critical for elimination

## Recommendations for MalariaSentinel Development Priority

1. **Immediate (M1-M2)**: Implement GEE-based environmental suitability pipeline (validated by Frake 2020) and Bayesian risk mapping (INLA/SPDE approach from Morlighem 2025). These are the highest-confidence, most directly replicable components.

2. **Near-term (M3-M4)**: Add intervention cascade module (Champagne 2025 framework) and multi-species vector parameterization (AnophelesModel R package). These extend existing ABM capability with validated methods.

3. **Medium-term (M5-M6)**: Integrate hydrological process modeling (Smith 2024 approach) and human mobility data. These are the highest-impact but least validated components — they require new development and local calibration.

4. **Ongoing**: Build Ghana-specific archetype calibration using the Nigeria EMOD approach (22 archetypes). This provides the foundation for all subsequent modeling.
