# MoVe: an integrated tool to explore the relationship between human mobility and vector-borne disease

**Authors:** Chaitawat Sa-ngamuang, Myat Su Yin, et al.
**Journal:** Scientific Reports | **Year:** 2026 | **DOI:** 10.1038/s41598-026-39007-3
**File:** papers/abm-geospatial/sa-ngamuang-et-al-2026-move-integrated-tool-human-mobility.md

## Abstract

This paper presents MoVe, an integrated platform combining mobility analysis with agent-based simulation for malaria transmission dynamics along the Thai-Myanmar border. The system enables exploratory data analysis, mobility metrics generation, and agent-based simulations parameterised by mobility patterns and spatial risk factors. Results demonstrate distinct mobility patterns into high-risk areas among different occupational groups, with implications for targeted intervention design.

## Methods

- Integrated platform architecture combining mobility data processing with ABM simulation
- Exploratory data analysis of human movement patterns using GPS and mobile phone data
- Agent-based simulation of malaria transmission incorporating mobility-driven exposure
- Occupational group stratification to identify differential risk exposure
- Spatial risk factor integration for transmission modelling

## Key Results

- Developed a unified platform that bridges mobility analysis and ABM simulation for vector-borne disease
- Distinct mobility patterns identified across occupational groups (e.g., farmers, traders, migrants)
- Occupational groups with higher exposure to high-risk areas showed elevated transmission potential
- Platform enables scenario testing for intervention strategies targeting mobile populations
- Demonstrated feasibility of integrating real-world mobility data into transmission models

## Relevance to MalariaSentinel (Centinela)

Directly applicable to MalariaSentinel's geospatial ABM capabilities. The MoVe platform demonstrates how human mobility data can be integrated into transmission models to capture spatial heterogeneity in exposure. For the Centinela's Ghana application, this approach could model how population movement between districts affects focal transmission patterns, particularly relevant for border areas and seasonal worker migration. The occupational stratification approach aligns with the Centinela's goal of targeted intervention delivery.

## Limitations

- Limited to Thai-Myanmar border context; generalisability to other settings untested
- Mobility data quality and completeness may vary across settings
- Computational scalability for large-scale applications not fully demonstrated
- Requires substantial baseline data infrastructure for mobility tracking

## Future Directions

- Validate against independent datasets from different endemic settings
- Incorporate real-time mobile phone data streams for dynamic mobility estimation
- Extend to multi-pathogen co-transmission scenarios
- Develop lightweight version for resource-limited deployment contexts
