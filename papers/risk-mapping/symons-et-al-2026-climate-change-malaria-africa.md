# Projected impacts of climate change on malaria in Africa

**Authors:** Tasmin L. Symons, Alexander Moran, et al.
**Journal:** Nature | **Year:** 2026 | **DOI:** 10.1038/s41586-025-10015-z
**File:** papers/risk-mapping/symons-et-al-2026-climate-change-malaria-africa.md

---

## Abstract

This study integrates 25 years of African data on climate, malaria burden, control interventions, socioeconomic factors, and extreme weather events to project future malaria impacts. Under current control levels, an estimated 123 million additional malaria cases and 532,000 additional deaths are projected between 2024–2050. Critically, extreme weather events account for 79% of additional cases, highlighting climate variability as a primary driver of future malaria burden.

## Methods

- Integration of 25 years of pan-African data: climate, malaria burden, control interventions, socioeconomic indicators
- Extreme weather event analysis (droughts, floods, heatwaves) and malaria associations
- Statistical modelling of climate-malaria relationships with control intervention covariates
- Scenario-based projections under current control levels (no additional intervention scaling)
- Sensitivity analysis to climate scenarios and intervention coverage
- Decomposition of additional burden into climate vs. control vs. socioeconomic drivers

## Key Results

- 123 million additional malaria cases projected (2024–2050) under current control levels
- 532,000 additional deaths projected over the same period
- Extreme weather events account for 79% of projected additional cases
- Current control interventions insufficient to offset climate-driven increases
- Socioeconomic development provides modest protective effect
- Sub-Saharan Africa bears disproportionate burden of climate-malaria impacts

## Relevance to MalariaSentinel (Centinela)

Critical climate-malaria projections for MalariaSentinel's predictive modeling. The finding that extreme weather drives 79% of additional cases directly informs the Centinela's climate resilience module. For Ghana, this means integrating flood and drought early warning data into transmission risk predictions. The 25-year dataset provides a validation benchmark for the Centinela's long-term projection capabilities. The decomposition of drivers (climate vs. control vs. socioeconomic) supports evidence-based resource allocation.

## Limitations

- Projections assume current control levels; additional intervention scaling would change outcomes
- National-level data may mask sub-national heterogeneity
- Extreme weather event attribution has inherent uncertainty
- Socioeconomic projections carry additional uncertainty

## Future Directions

- Develop sub-national projections for Ghana specifically
- Integrate with ABM for mechanistic transmission modelling under climate scenarios
- Add intervention scaling scenarios to assess adaptation options
- Create real-time climate-malaria monitoring dashboard
