# Future malaria environmental suitability in Africa is sensitive to hydrology

**Authors:** Smith MW, Willis T, et al.
**Journal:** Science | **Year:** 2024 | **DOI:** 10.1126/science.adk8755
**File:** papers/risk-mapping/smith-et-al-2024-hydrology-sensitive-malaria.md

---

## Abstract

This study applies a validated ensemble of global hydrological and climate models to estimate malaria transmission suitability across Africa. The analysis finds a net decrease in suitable areas from 2025 onward, with greater sensitivity to greenhouse gas emissions than previously recognised. Hydrology-based estimates show more complex transmission patterns than precipitation-only models, demonstrating that hydrological processes (runoff, groundwater, soil moisture) are critical determinants of malaria risk.

## Methods

- Ensemble of global hydrological models (GHMs) and climate models (GCMs)
- Environmental suitability framework incorporating hydrological variables beyond precipitation
- Comparison of hydrology-based vs. precipitation-only suitability estimates
- Greenhouse gas emission scenario analysis (SSP2-4.5, SSP5-8.5)
- Spatial analysis of suitability change projections across African regions
- Validation against observed malaria distribution and entomological data

## Key Results

- Net decrease in environmentally suitable areas from 2025 onward under most scenarios
- Hydrology-based estimates reveal more complex transmission patterns than precipitation-only models
- Greater sensitivity to greenhouse gas emissions than previously estimated
- Runoff, groundwater, and soil moisture are critical intermediate variables
- Some regions show increased suitability while others decrease — spatially heterogeneous response

## Relevance to MalariaSentinel (Centinela)

Demonstrates importance of hydrological processes in malaria risk mapping. For the Centinela's Ghana application, this means incorporating hydrological models (not just rainfall) into environmental suitability mapping. The Volta Basin's complex hydrology (dams, irrigation, seasonal flooding) makes hydrology-sensitive modelling particularly relevant. The finding of net suitability decrease from 2025 onward provides a baseline projection for Ghana's long-term elimination planning.

## Limitations

- Global-scale models may not capture local hydrological complexity
- Hydrological model uncertainty is substantial
- Does not capture human modification of hydrology (dams, irrigation)
- Transmission suitability ≠ actual transmission (ignores vector competence, human immunity)

## Future Directions

- Apply hydrology-sensitive framework to Ghana/Volta Basin specifically
- Incorporate local hydrological infrastructure (Akosombo Dam, irrigation schemes)
- Couple with ABM for mechanistic transmission modelling
- Validate against Ghanaian entomological and epidemiological data
